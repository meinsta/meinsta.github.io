Welp, along the rabbit hole I came across two articles that brought some clarity to the Turing-Shannon relationship.

The first article, published in March 1950 in Philosophical Magazine by Claude Shannon entitled Programming a Computer for Playing Chess (source) was the first article about computer chess. Sound familiar?

The second paper is from Alan Turing, Chess, published in Faster Than Thought just three years later in 1953, and is a hallmark in digital computing writing and discourse. Here is Faster Than Thought for sale on a publishing site for around 389 USD (source):

It took a bit of digging to find some concrete connections between these contemporaries. It's still hard to say if these programming theses were conceived precisely in the Bell Labs, but we can say for certain that Shannon and Turing must have at least had correspondences. How else can we explain Turing and Shannon's obsession with chess as a template for computing within the same decade that they both worked at the same facilities?
